ED Patient Hold Decision Guide

A simplified guide to Tennessee patient holds for ED physicians.

Initial Assessment: What is the primary cause of the patient's condition?

Regarding Drugs or Alcohol Intoxication: A patient incapacitated by **acute drug or alcohol intoxication** has a medical/toxicological condition. This requires a **Medical Incapacity (Title 68)** evaluation, NOT a Psychiatric (Title 33 / "6401") Hold. The psychiatric hold is reserved for cases where the danger is caused by an underlying psychiatric illness (e.g., schizophrenia, major depression).

This is the most critical decision point. The patient's presentation dictates the correct legal pathway. The two pathways—Psychiatric vs. Medical—are legally distinct and mutually exclusive. Choose the path that best describes the root cause of the patient's incapacity or dangerousness.

Psychiatric Hold Protocol (TCA Title 33)

This pathway is a formal legal process that initiates a potential court-ordered commitment. It requires strict adherence to statutory criteria documented on Form MH-5542.

Step 1: The "6401" Emergency Detention Criteria

To initiate an emergency detention for evaluation, you must have reason to believe BOTH of the following are true. Click each item to confirm.

The person has a mental illness or serious emotional disturbance.

(Includes psychiatric disorders, alcohol dependence, or drug dependence. Does NOT include intellectual disabilities.)

The person poses an imminent substantial likelihood of serious harm BECAUSE OF the mental illness.

(Harm includes threats/attempts of suicide/homicide, or inability to avoid severe impairment from specific risks.)

Step 2: The "6404" Certificate of Need Criteria

If the "6401" criteria are met, your examination must support ALL FOUR of these points to complete the first Certificate of Need for admission.

✓

1. Has a mental illness or serious emotional disturbance.

✓

2. Poses imminent substantial likelihood of serious harm BECAUSE OF the illness.

✓

3. Needs care, training, or treatment BECAUSE OF the illness.

✓

4. All available less drastic alternatives are unsuitable.

Action Required: You must be able to document specific facts supporting all checked items on Form MH-5542. If you cannot confirm all required criteria, this is not a valid psychiatric hold. Re-evaluate if the Medical Incapacity pathway is more appropriate.

The Journey of a Psychiatric Hold

Initiating a hold is the first step in a formal legal process. Here is the typical sequence of events.

1

ED Physician Initiates Hold

The ED Physician documents the "6401" criteria on Part 1 of Form MH-5542, then completes the first "6404" Certificate of Need on Part 2 after examination.

2

Transport to Psychiatric Facility

Patient is transported with the completed form to a psychiatric hospital or treatment resource for a second evaluation.

3

Second Certificate of Need

A second, independent qualified professional at the receiving facility examines the patient. If they concur, they complete the 2nd Certificate of Need (Part 3 of Form MH-5542).

4

Notice to the Court

With two certificates, the psychiatric facility files a Notice of Admission (Form MH-5098) with the General Sessions Court, formally starting the judicial process.

5

Judicial Review & Order

A judge reviews the forms and, if there is probable cause, issues an Ex Parte Order (Form MH-5099) to hold the patient, appoint counsel, and set a court date.

Medical Incapacity Protocol (TCA Title 68)

This pathway is used when a patient cannot make decisions due to a medical condition. The goal is not court commitment, but obtaining lawful consent for treatment from a surrogate. This is the basis for a "Medical Hold".

A "Medical Hold" is legally justified by two components: 1) A documented finding of incapacity, and 2) Documented consent from a legal surrogate.

Physician's Duty Checklist

Follow these steps to legally manage a medically incapacitated patient. Proper documentation in the medical record is essential.

  • 1. Determine and Document Incapacity

    Perform a capacity assessment. Document in the medical record your specific findings that the patient cannot understand the risks, benefits, and alternatives to care.

  • 2. Identify a Surrogate Decision-Maker

    Follow the legal hierarchy to find a surrogate. Document all attempts to contact them.

  • 3. Obtain and Document Informed Consent

    Once a surrogate is identified, explain the situation and obtain their consent for admission and treatment. Document this consent conversation in the medical record. This provides the legal basis to hold the patient.

  • 4. If No Surrogate is Reasonably Available...

    The law provides a backup. You MUST do one of the following: A) Get concurrence from a second, independent physician, OR B) Consult the hospital's ethics committee. Document this consultation.

Surrogate Decision-Maker Hierarchy

You must follow this order of priority when seeking a surrogate. You cannot skip a level unless no one in that category is reasonably available.

1. Health Care Agent (in a durable power of attorney for health care)

2. Judicially Appointed Guardian

3. Spouse (unless legally separated)

4. Adult Child

5. Parent

6. Adult Sibling

7. Other Adult Relative

8. Other Adult with special care/concern for patient

Section 1: The Psychiatric Involuntary Commitment Framework (Tennessee Code Title 33)

Navigating the management of patients with acute psychiatric crises in the emergency department (ED) requires a precise understanding of a legal framework distinct from general medical care. In Tennessee, this process is governed by Title 33 of the Tennessee Code Annotated (TCA), which establishes a formal, multi-stage legal proceeding designed to protect the due process rights of the individual while allowing for necessary emergency intervention. This framework is not merely a clinical pathway but a legal one, initiated by specific forms and culminating in judicial oversight. Misunderstanding or misapplying these procedures can lead to significant legal liability for clinicians and their institutions. The process begins with an emergency detention for the purpose of evaluation and, if specific criteria are met, proceeds to a certification for involuntary admission.

1.1 The Gateway to Evaluation: The § 33-6-401 Emergency Detention ("6401 Hold")

The initial step in the emergency involuntary commitment process is the emergency detention, commonly referred to as a "6401 hold." This action is authorized under TCA § 33-6-401 and serves a single, specific purpose: to detain an individual against their will for the immediate purpose of a professional examination to determine if they require emergency involuntary admission. It is the legal gateway to a formal psychiatric assessment.

Critically, the statute establishes a strict, two-part legal test that must be met before a 6401 hold can be initiated. Both conditions are mandatory, and the presence of one without the other is insufficient to justify the detention.

  • Presence of a Mental Illness or Serious Emotional Disturbance: The initiator must have reason to believe the person has a "mental illness" or "serious emotional disturbance." The statutory definition of "mental illness" is broad, encompassing "a psychiatric disorder, alcohol dependence, or drug dependence." However, the statute explicitly clarifies that this definition does not include intellectual or other developmental disabilities.
  • Imminent Harm Because of the Mental Illness: The person must pose an "imminent substantial likelihood of serious harm" as defined in TCA § 33-6-501. This harm must be a direct consequence of the mental illness or serious emotional disturbance. This causal link is the most crucial, and often most misunderstood, element of the standard. It is not enough for a patient to have a mental illness and separately be a danger; the danger must arise from the illness.

1.2 The Certification for Admission: The § 33-6-404 Certificate of Need ("6404")

If the 6401 hold is the legal authorization to "detain for evaluation," the Certificate of Need (CON), or "6404," is the legal certification that results from that evaluation. It is the formal, professional opinion, documented on Form MH-5542, that the patient meets the statutory criteria for emergency involuntary admission. The first CON is typically completed in the ED and requires the professional to certify that four distinct criteria have been met:

  1. The patient has a mental illness or serious emotional disturbance.
  2. The patient poses an imminent substantial likelihood of serious harm because of the mental illness.
  3. The patient needs care, training, or treatment because of the mental illness.
  4. All available less drastic alternatives to placement in a hospital or treatment resource are unsuitable to meet the needs of the person.

1.3 From ED to Courtroom: The Full Involuntary Admission Process

The sequence of forms and procedures required under Title 33 makes it clear that an emergency involuntary commitment is not a private clinical matter. The ED physician's actions are the first, crucial steps in a formal judicial process that is carefully structured to protect the patient's constitutional rights to due process. The entire workflow is designed to move from a clinical setting to a legal one, with increasing levels of scrutiny, culminating in a judge's order to hold the patient.

Section 2: Managing the Medically Incapacitated Patient (Tennessee Code Title 68)

A frequent and high-risk point of confusion in emergency medicine is how to legally manage a patient who lacks decision-making capacity due to a purely medical condition—such as severe intoxication, delirium from sepsis, metabolic encephalopathy, or a head injury—and wishes to leave against medical advice (AMA). The crucial determination for clinicians and hospital administrators is that the Title 33 psychiatric commitment process is the incorrect and legally indefensible framework for these patients. The correct and sole legal authority for this scenario is found in a completely different section of Tennessee law: Title 68, the Tennessee Health Care Decisions Act (HCDA).

2.1 The Misapplication of Title 33: Why a 6401/6404 is Improper for Purely Medical Incapacity

Using the Title 33 involuntary commitment process for a patient whose incapacity stems from a non-psychiatric medical condition is a serious legal error. The plain language of TCA § 33-6-401 requires that the "imminent substantial likelihood of serious harm" be because of a "mental illness or serious emotional disturbance". To complete a 6401/6404 form for a patient with delirium from a UTI, a physician would have to certify that the patient's condition is due to a mental illness, which is factually incorrect. This action constitutes making a false certification on a legal document and exposes the physician and the hospital to significant legal liability.

2.2 The Correct Legal Authority: The Tennessee Health Care Decisions Act (HCDA)

The proper legal pathway for making treatment decisions for an adult patient who is medically incapacitated is the Tennessee Health Care Decisions Act (HCDA). The law itself builds a firewall between the two statutory schemes, reinforcing that clinicians must choose one path or the other; they cannot be used interchangeably.

2.4 Securing Lawful Consent: The Surrogate Decision-Maker Hierarchy

Once a physician has determined that a patient lacks capacity, they have a legal duty under the HCDA to obtain informed consent from a legally authorized surrogate decision-maker. The HCDA establishes a mandatory "waterfall" or hierarchy of priority for identifying this surrogate:

  1. Health Care Agent (in an advance directive)
  2. Judicially Appointed Guardian
  3. Spouse (unless legally separated)
  4. Adult Child
  5. Parent
  6. Adult Sibling
  7. Other Adult Relative
  8. Other Adult with special care and concern for the patient

2.6 The "Medical Hold": A Practical Application of Law

The term "medical hold" is a piece of clinical jargon, not a legal term. It is the practical term for the lawful act of preventing a medically incapacitated patient from leaving the hospital against medical advice. A medical hold is legally justified by the combination of two documented facts: (1) A well-documented assessment by a physician that the patient lacks decision-making capacity, and (2) Lawful informed consent for admission and treatment obtained from the appropriate surrogate decision-maker.

Section 3: Navigating the Complexities of Acute Intoxication

Patients presenting to the ED with acute intoxication represent one of the most challenging and legally ambiguous populations. A primary source of confusion stems from the statutory definition of "mental illness" in Title 33, which includes "alcohol dependence" and "drug dependence". A clinician might incorrectly conclude that a 6401 psychiatric hold is the appropriate tool.

This interpretation is generally incorrect and legally perilous. In the vast majority of cases involving acute intoxication, the patient's confusion, poor judgment, and lack of capacity are the direct, transient, pharmacological effects of the substance itself. This is a toxicological state, a medical condition, not a manifestation of the chronic disease of "dependence". Therefore, the proper legal framework is almost always medical incapacity under the Title 68 Health Care Decisions Act.
